Abstract: Dent resistance of automobile body panels is an important property for automobile design and manufacture, but the study on 
this aspect is not still profound. This study is to summarize the testing methods and physical significations of static and dynamic dent 
resistance of automobile body panels combined with the author’s study, and to analyze the dent behaviors in the round. Several 
influence factors on dent resistance are expatiated including the mechanical properties of materials, stress states after forming, bake 
hardening ability, modulus, methods of testing, and structure of specimens and so on. The automotive lightweight and application of 
high strength steel sheets and aluminum alloys sheets are analyzed, and the significance of testing of dent resistance, especially for 
dynamic dent resistance of auto-panels, and the finite element simulation analysis are emphasized. To explain the physical phenomenon 
of dent behaviors, the latest and concerned study results are also discussed. According to this study, a dent resistance test and evaluation 
standard of Society of Automotive Engineers of China for automotive body panel is presented and is being carried out, and an industry 
conference is hold to discuss the working-out of the standard, a primary schedule of this standard is confirmed now. The study can guide 
the further testing and study of dent resistant of auto-panels.  
 
Key words: dent resistance, dent resistance test, automobile body panels, dynamic dent resistance, stiffness

2  Definition and Testing Methods 
 
Because the responses to high speed impact and 
quasi-static loads are different, dent resistance is divided 
into static and dynamic dent resistance. Static dent 
reistance is the resistance against permanent deformation 
due to static forces, and it mainly relates to local loads; 
Dynamic dent reistance is the resistance against permanent 
deformation due to high speed impact (dynamic conditions), 
and it mainly relates to the applied kinetic energy and 
momentum. Both dent resistances are important evaluating 
indicators. Based on such a classification criterion, the DR 
testing is also divided into static dent reistance testing and 
dynamic dent reistance testing.

The dent resistance test is a process with both elastic 
and plastic deformations. The stiffness can be determined 
from the elastic portion of sheet or parts during dent test. 
Initial stiffness is determined from the slope of the initial 
linear portion of the load vs. deflection curve, and

second linear portion of the load vs. deflection curve of a 
given panel. The panel stiffness is normally measured using 
a flat headed punch of diameter 100 mm. It is shown in Ref. 
[5] that the stiffness determined using the 25.4 mm 
spherical denting punch was exactly the same or almost 
equal to that determined by the flat-headed stiffness 
measurement punch with diameter 100 mm. EKSTRAND,

of panels against elastic deflection.

3.2  Geometry and curvature 
The geometry affects strongly the DR of the panels 
which are lower yield stress and/or lower thickness 
specimen. The influence of geometry and curvature on 
dynamic DR is less than on static DR. Under the same 
dynamic testing conditions, the dent force is the lowest for 
the dome specimen (10.2 mm cup height) and the highest 
for the flat specimen with prestrain which is produced from 
the Marciniak cup specimen with approximately 5.5% for 
major strain and 1.5% minor strain[6], the lower dent force 
from the dome specimen is due to its higher initial stiffness 
and lower amount of pre-strain. In the same way, geometry 
and curvature affects also the stiffness of the panels, 
theoretically, the initial stiffness under a static concentrated 
load is proportional to E·tn, where E is elastic modulus, t is 
thickness of panels, the exponent n depends the curvature 
of the panel, with a decrease of the radius of curvature from 
1.0 m to 0.4 mm, n=1.6–2.7[2]. It shows that the static 
stiffness depends less on thickness for the panels which 
have high radius of curvature. A result indicates also that 
the stiffness decrease as the radius of panel curvature 
increase, however, when the radii is enough higher, the 
panel stiffness does not is almost invariable.  
Fig. 6 shows the influence of different curvatures on

figure, dynamic DR increases when static DR increases, but 
the correlation depends on panel curvature. DANIEL, et al, 
[2] suggested, due to the differences in elastic dent panel

3.4  Testing conditions  
SHI, et al,[12] attained the conclusion that at high strain 
rate, the difference of dynamic DR is smaller for different 
yield strength steel than that of static DR. At the same time, 
this conclusion also means that the dynamic DR is less 
dependent on the yield strength of material than the static 
DR, the difference of dynamic DR of high strength steels 
and mild steels is smaller than that of static DR. So 
considering the dynamic DR of materials, the sheet 
thickness and geometry may be more important. But 
DYLAN, et al,[10] considered that increased panels’ 
thickness not always aided dynamic DR, in certain 
situations increasing sheet thickness will hinder dynamic 
DR, which is described in Fig. 7. Fig. 7 demonstrates that 
when the impact speed is higher than 4m/s, the dent depth 
of 0.81 mm specimen is lower than that of 0.93 and 1.00 
mm specimens. This is a special phenomenon and it is 
needed to be discussed deeply. SHIH, et al,[4] tested 
dynamic DR of the DQSK (drawing quality special killed 
steel), BH steel and DP steel at 1.6–6.4 km/h impact speeds. 
The authors discovered that the dent load slightly increases 
with speed up to 4.8 km/h and reduces at the speed of 6.4 
km/h. A panel-deflecting model consisting of three stages 
were proposed in Refs. [4, 13], to explain the relationship 
between dent depth and impact energy, and it was verified 
by the strain measurements.

of the nodal point is restricted by the existence of the panel 
wall. The energy consumption for the movement of the 
nodal point of deflection no longer increases and the 
portion used for plastic deformation increases, which 
results in a greater dent depth. As expected, this might 
happen to the results at enough higher impact speed.   
According to a panel-deflecting model consisting of

That’s necessary to study further by far.

4  Relationship Between DR and Stiffness 
 
Automotive panel stiffness is known to influence the DR 
of the panels, and a lot of studies regarding the dent and 
stiffness of automotive panels are reported[7–10], some with 
contradictory results. YUTORI[7] concluded that a panel 
would have good static DR if increasing the panel stiffness 
or the panel could support a large load prior to permanent 
indentation; but DICELLO, et al,[8] attained a conclusion 
that decreased panel stiffness aided static dent resistance;  
another conclusion was attained by Ref. [9] that for a panel 
with low stiffness it was beneficial to reduce the stiffness, 
whereas for a panel with a high stiffness it was beneficial to 
increase the stiffness in order to improve the dent resistance. 
There seems to be no simple relation between the stiffness 
and the dent resistance. DYLAN, et al,[10] performed a deep 
analysis about this. DYLAN considered th

5  Design of Automobile and Simulation of 
Dent Resistance 
 
Dent, though very small, is easily detected by the human 
eyes, leading to dissatisfaction with the sense of quality of 
the car. Therefore, there are specific requirements regarding 
dent resistance of exterior automotive panels. However, it 
is difficult to carry out a quantitative analysis of dent 
resistance without dent testing; in fact, just initial stiffness 
can be calculated by equation, and most other influences of 
parameters 
on 
dent 
resistance 
only 
are 
analyzed 
qualitatively. DR of outer panels, such as doors, hoods and 
lids are normally determined by physical testing. However, 
this testing procedure has several drawbacks. It is both 
costly and time consuming but most importantly, it cannot 
be carried out until rather late in the car project, in general 
towards the end of the design process. By then, most of the 
factors governing the stiffness and dent properties are 
already specified. Should the panel fail to meet the 
requirements, little can be done to improve the panel’s 
performance. Thus, it is obvious that a dent resistance 
prediction of auto-body panel at an early stage in the design 
process is required. It is interesting to know how the DR 
can be predicted at an early stage of design process; in fact 
finite element simulation of DR of panels and parts is a 
good method and significant for exterior automotive panels 
design and physical testing optimization.  
In 1980 YUTORI[7] carried out a finite element model 
(FEM) simulation of static dent. This may be one of the 
earliest examples of a FEM simulation of static dent, but 
the model used only 30 axi-symmetric elements and the 
indenter was described as a point load. Following Yutori’s 
study, many researchers carried on the FEM simulation 
analysis of DR. In 1993, WERNER[9] showed a comparison 
between different finite element(FE) programs based on 
implicit as well as explicit technique, including MARC, 
ABAQUS, and LS-DYNA. This was the first time a 
meshed indenter was applied to instead of a point load. 
However, Werner obtained undesired dynamic effects 
leading to poor correlation with the experimental results. 
SABBAGH, et al,[14] predicted static dent depths in actual 
auto-body panels in 1995, two doors were modeled 
considering several dent locations, and one of the doors 
was assembled including the inner and outer panel. 
Sabbagh’s work was also the first time to include local 
refinement of the finite element mesh in the dent region in 
order to gain sufficient resolution. And the material 
stress-strain data used was attained from tensile sections cut 
from existing doors. In 1996, CHAVALI continued the

7  Conclusions 
 
(1) Dent resistance of auto-panels have become an 
important requirement of automotive covers and one of key 
design parameter of panels, and the study of dent resistance 
should be regarded. 
(2) Dent resistance includes static and dynamic dent 
resistance. 
Dynamic 
loading 
conditions 
are 
more 
realistically reflected in the real life situations that cause 
denting, so understanding of dynamic denting behavior of 
automotive panels is greater important than that of the 
static denting behavior. 
(3) The test method or methods are not standardized, so a 
comparison of the values obtained by different methods 
causes some problems, and lots of woks should be done in 
order to standardize the testing methods. 
(4) The dent resistance increases when the yield strength, 
bake hardening ability, and curvature increase, of course 
the thickness also, an increase of 80 MPa of aluminum 
yield stress reduces the dent depth by 25%, and in the range 
of 0.9–1.2 mm sheet thickness for static dent resistance of 
aluminum panels, a yield strength increase of 30 MPa is 
necessary to compensate for a thickness reduction of 0.1 
mm. But increased panels’ thickness not always aids 
dynamic dent resistance especially for dynamic dent under 
higher impact speeds testing conditions. The stiffness and 
dynamic dent resistance of aluminum alloys and steels is 
different distinctly, due to their modulus and strain rate 
sensitivity is different. During the testing, the specimen 
geometry, testing methods, and testing conditions affect the 
results of dent test.  
(5) Finite element simulation of dent resistance of 
auto-panels and parts should be developed further, 
especially for simulation of dynamic dent resistance. 
(6) It is beneficial to increase the stiffness of auto-panels 
in order to improve the dent resistance.
