Ⅱ. 结论与建议 Conclusions and Suggestions 
结论Conclusions： 
1. The heatshield has failed through fatigue, mainly originating from areas of fretting wear both between the two steel 
panels and at contact points between the fastener/clamped member and panel. 
2. The panel quality is poor.  Folds in the metal give rise to fretting between panels, and ripples prevented good contact 
between the clamped members, allowing excessive movement and vibration. 
3. The glass fibre interlayer was missing from this sample.  However, even if correctly included, the fastened joints are 
not robust and opportunities for fretting at the panel-fastener interface are still present. 
4. The material tensile strength of both panels meet the requirement. 
 
建议Suggestions： 
1. Ensure components meet the design requirements.  Glass fibre matting must be installed and steel panels must 
be free from folds, ripples etc. 
2. Improve joint design to reduce point-contact and prevent excessive movement between panels. 
3. Consider increasing the panel thickness, which would both improve joint stiffness, and reduce the effect of 
vibration in the part.

III. 调查结果 Investigation Results  
The heatshield was received as shown in figure 1.  The failure mode was cracking around the mounting bolt on 
the front, additional cracks were found near to the mounting bolts on the rear mounting flange, and also at the end 
arch.  The part was generally poorly formed with extensive folding, creases and tooling abrasions. Webs around 
the turbine housing area were not formed at all.  The part was marked with a Quality Control sticker.  
An excerpt from the drawing is shown in figure 2. The design structure of the heatshield being two layers of 
0.4mm low carbon steel, with a fibreglass matting interlayer, however, no evidence of fibreglass was found in the 
part.  The fasteners were not submitted for analysis.  The fractures are discussed individually on the following 
pages.

Fracture – Front mounting point 
The two steel layers had both cracked around the entire mounting point.  The outer surface of the outer panel showed 
traces of zinc-flake coating from the bolt head, which is evidence of vibration movement between the bolt and the panel.  
The crack had mainly propagated around the circumference of the bolt, from positions A-C-D as shown in figure 3a.  A 
further crack was initiated from a heavy scratch, which, combined with a crease, initiated a crack in the inner layer.

Crack E 
 
The steel panels at the end of the heatshield are folded tightly, forming significant stress concentration and panel thinning.  
On separating the two pieces, fretting was visible within the fold, as well as small secondary cracks, suggesting the 
fretting wear as primary origin.  However, fracture analysis with both optical microscopy and SEM revealed heavy 
wear, damage and oxidation to the fracture surface (figure 7), thus it was not possible to completely exclude the panel 
edge as a crack origin.

has resulted in only a few contact points between the fixing element and panel, resulting in fretting wear between 
fastener and panel and also between the two panels (figures 8b, c, d).  Figure 9a shows secondary cracking at the fretted 
area, indicating this as the origin of the main crack.  A further crack was also found, originating at another area of 
fretting wear (figure 9b).
